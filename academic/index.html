PhD Thesis · Writing Studies (Mythology & Screen Media)

Remaking Mythology:

European and Mediterranean Cosmologies on Screen

This project places cosmology—creation, order, and ending—at the centre of mythic analysis. It traces cosmological ideas from oral tradition and manuscript culture, through literary reinterpretations, into contemporary screen media. Rather than rehearse the hero’s journey, it asks how film, television, and games act as mythopoesis: preserving ancient frameworks, reshaping them for modern sensibilities, and revealing how providence and fate, harmony and enchantment, covenant and resurrection, and cosmic indifference still animate the imagination.

Supervisor: A/Prof Frances Di Lauro · Discipline of English and Writing · University of Sydney

Thesis Overview

Mode & Scope

Traditional dissertation (~80,000 words). The study proceeds in three stages: (1) Original Contexts (primary cosmological sources), (2) Literary/Theological Reinterpretations (Shakespeare, Tolkien, Gaiman, Stoker, Lovecraft), and (3) Cinematic & Post‑Film Adaptations (films, TV, games), establishing a genealogy from antiquity to screen culture.

Abstract

European and Mediterranean cosmologies persist and transform across media. Using four interpretive pairings—Providence & Wyrd (Catholic/Medieval Christian and Germanic/Anglo‑Saxon), Harmony & Enchantment (Greco‑Roman and Celtic), Law, Covenant & Resurrection (Hebrew and Egyptian), and Chaos & Indifference (Classical and Enlightenment)—the dissertation analyses how cosmologies move from text to screen. Representative works are read in their original contexts, in literary transmissions, and in film, TV, and interactive titles. By combining comparative mythology, genealogical analysis, and symbolic interpretation of narrative, image, and music, the project argues that cinema and screen media function as modern mythopoesis.

Interpretive Pairings

  • Providence & Wyrd — Christian providence and Germanic fate in tension; e.g., The Northman, The Last Kingdom, and Tolkien on screen.
  • Harmony & Enchantment — Platonic harmony and Celtic Otherworld; from Theogony and Timaeus to Shakespeare and modern fantasy.
  • Law, Covenant & Resurrection — Hebrew apocalyptic and Egyptian rebirth; covenantal narratives and resurrective motifs in modern retellings.
  • Chaos & Indifference — From Hesiod and Lucretius to Lovecraft; cosmic indifference in contemporary horror and sci‑fi (e.g., Underwater).

Methods

  • Comparative & Genealogical — tracing cosmological ideas across traditions and epochs.
  • Adaptation & Intermediality — literature as adapter; screen media as further adaptation.
  • Symbolic/Semiotic Reading — close analysis of narrative, visual motifs, and film/game music (leitmotif, timbre, mix aesthetics).

Aims & Objectives

  • Analyse cosmological pairings in primary sources (Norse, Celtic, Greco‑Roman, Hebrew, Egyptian, Christian).
  • Examine transmissions via literary/theological reinterpretations into modern culture.
  • Investigate how film/TV/games adapt, reframe, or secularise cosmology through story, image, and sound.
  • Assess continuity and transformation across stages; argue for screen media as mythopoesis.

Expected Contributions

  • A comparative framework centred on cosmology (beyond the hero’s journey).
  • A diachronic genealogy from oral/manuscript to contemporary screen culture.
  • Integration of mythology/theology with adaptation and media analysis; emphasis on music as cosmological signifier.

Proposed Work Plan

  • Year 1 — Foundations: research questions; literature review; close reading of primary texts; draft Intro/Lit Review/Methodology.
  • Year 2 — Analysis: original & literary/theological contexts; begin case‑study chapters (Providence–Wyrd; Harmony–Enchantment); present preliminary findings.
  • Year 3 — Synthesis: complete remaining pairings (Law–Covenant–Resurrection; Chaos–Indifference); draft synthesis; revise and submit; prepare article.

Corpus & Case Studies (indicative)

Film & Television

  • The Lord of the Rings (Jackson) — Christian/Germanic synthesis and myth‑structural scoring (Shore).
  • The Passion of the Christ (Gibson); The Tree of Life (Malick) — providence, creation, eschaton.
  • The Northman (Eggers) — wyrd, ritual, vengeance cosmology.
  • Classical & Egyptian lines via Agora, Gods of Egypt, Prince of Egypt, and mummy cycles.
  • Cosmic indifference in Underwater, Color Out of Space.

Games (Screen‑adjacent Media)

  • God of War (2018, Ragnarök) — Norse cosmology and fatherhood theology.
  • Assassin’s Creed (Valhalla, Odyssey, Origins) — historical mythopoesis across Norse/Greco/Egyptian frames.
  • Hellblade (I & II) — vision, rite, and sound as cosmological pressure.

Publications & Works in Progress

  • “Leitmotif and Law: Reframing Ritual Authority through Score in Contemporary Medievalist Film” — in preparation.
  • “From Folk Timbres to Synth Monoliths: Timbre as Theology in Game Worlds” — article planning.

Book Project — Wyrdcasters: Chaos in a Dying Realm
