Proposed PhD Thesis · English & Writing Studies (Anglo‑Saxon and Adaptation)

The Cosmologies of Anglo‑Saxon England:
From Manuscript to Screen

This project investigates how Anglo‑Saxon cosmology—creation, order, fate, and ending—was imagined in Old English poetry and Alfredian translations; transmitted through manuscript and medieval theology; reinterpreted in later literature; and remade in modern screen culture. Rather than rehearse ‘the hero’s journey’, it treats cinema, television, and games as mythopoesis, showing how providence and wyrd, covenant and law, harmony and enchantment, and visions of apocalypse are adapted for contemporary audiences.

Thesis Overview

Mode & Scope

Traditional dissertation (~80,000 words). The study proceeds in three stages: (1) Original Contexts—Old English biblical poetry, heroic/elegiac verse, and Alfredian translations; (2) Transmission & Reception—medieval theology, manuscript culture, and later literature (esp. Tolkien and Alfredian historical fiction); (3) Screen Adaptation—film, television, and games as contemporary myth‑making media.

Abstract

Anglo‑Saxon cosmologies arose from a synthesis of Germanic fatalism, Christian providence and apocalypse, Celtic otherworlds, classical philosophy refracted through Latin authors, and Hebrew visions of covenant and law. This dissertation traces those frameworks from their articulation in Old English poetry and Alfred’s translations, through medieval transmission and literary reinterpretation, into modern screen culture where narrative, imagery, and music re‑stage them for new audiences.

Central Questions

  • How did Anglo‑Saxon England conceive creation, order, fate, and ending— and why?
  • Through what processes were these cosmologies transmitted, adapted, and reimagined across time?
  • How do film, television, and games remake these frameworks for the modern imagination?

Methodology

  • Textual & Philological: close readings of Beowulf, The Wanderer, Genesis B, Exodus, Daniel, and Alfred’s Boethius.
  • Comparative Contextualisation: dialogues with Celtic myth/Insular Christianity; Greco‑Roman cosmology; Hebrew scripture in vernacular poetry.
  • Reception & Adaptation: Tolkien’s legendarium; Alfredian historical fiction; intertextual and genealogical mapping.
  • Film & Media Analysis: narrative and symbolic analysis of screen texts; cinema as mythopoesis.
  • Sound & Atmosphere: how leitmotif, timbre, and mix aesthetics encode cosmological meaning.
  • Interactive Media: re‑imagining cosmology in playable form and performative space.
  • Law, Sovereignty, and Order: reading legal‑political structures (Roman law, covenant theology, Alfred’s reforms) as cosmological categories.

Aims & Objectives

  • Analyse Anglo‑Saxon cosmology in its textual matrix and cultural synthesis.
  • Trace transmission from manuscript to modern literature and screen.
  • Evaluate continuity/rupture and the role of music in mediating cosmology.
  • Argue for screen culture as a living engine of mythopoesis.

Film & Television

Games (Screen‑adjacent Media)

  • Kingdom Come: Deliverance; Assassin’s Creed: Valhalla; God of War (2018, Ragnarök); Hellblade I & II.
